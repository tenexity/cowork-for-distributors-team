# Price increase letter template

Use this when a manufacturer has raised prices and you need to pass it through to your contractor base. One letter, one page, sent in advance of the effective date. Keep it under 200 words — most contractors will skim.

---

## The letter

### Header

- **To:** [Contractor customer base — can be segmented by branch, by product line affected, or sent to all]
- **From:** [Your name, title / "The team at [Company name]"]
- **Date:** [Letter date]
- **Effective date of change:** [When the new pricing applies]

### The letter itself

**Subject (for email version):** [Product line] pricing update effective [date]

---

[Opening — direct, no preamble:]

[Manufacturer] announced a [X]% increase across the [product family] line, effective [date]. Here's what that means for you.

**What's changing:**
- [Specific line / SKU group / product category]
- [Effective date]
- [Typical impact — "about $[X] per unit on [typical SKU]" or "roughly [X]% across most SKUs"]

**What's not changing:**
- [Any carve-outs — contract pricing, job quotes already issued, special program pricing still honoured]
- [If applicable: "quotes issued before [date] hold at current pricing through [date]"]

**How to hold current pricing:**
- [Lock-in terms — "orders placed and shipped by [date] get current pricing"]
- [Stock position we're carrying — "we're holding deeper stock through [date] for anyone who wants to stock ahead"]
- [Any other protective move — "we're extending quote validity on open jobs by 30 days"]

**What you need to do:**
- Nothing required — this is a heads-up so you can plan
- If you have jobs bidding in the next [X] weeks, let your rep know and we'll lock your pricing at current rates while you work the bid

**Questions:** [Rep name, direct phone, direct email] — or reach any of our branches at [main line].

---

[Sign-off — first name alone or first name + title, no "Sincerely yours"]

## Versions of this letter

### Version A: Full customer base, single product line

One email, one line affected, sent to everyone. Use for a standard annual-type increase on a line most contractors use.

### Version B: Segmented by contractor type

HVAC contractors only, plumbing contractors only, service plumbers vs. builders. Use when the increase affects a line that skews strongly to one customer segment.

### Version C: Key-account personal outreach

Top 25 accounts get a call or a personal email from their rep, not the mass communication. Use for increases that hit a top account's core spend by more than a few thousand dollars — they deserve the courtesy of a conversation.

### Version D: Lock-in campaign

When the increase is large enough to drive real stock-ahead behaviour, turn the letter into a short campaign: the letter, then a follow-up list-of-SKUs offer, then a deadline-reminder.

## What good and bad look like

**Bad — the corporate-speak version:**
"Dear Valued Partner, We are writing to inform you that effective [date], Carrier will be implementing a pricing adjustment across their residential HVAC product line. This adjustment reflects ongoing market dynamics and rising input costs. We appreciate your continued partnership as we navigate these industry-wide challenges together. Should you have any questions or concerns, please do not hesitate to reach out."

**Good — the trade-fluent version:**
"Tom — Carrier announced a 7% increase on the 24-series and 25-series condensers, effective May 1. On a typical 3-ton unit, that's about $180 in cost. Orders placed and shipped before May 1 hold at current pricing. If you have jobs bidding in the next 60 days, Sarah can lock your pricing on open quotes. Questions — call her at 518-555-0142."

The good version is 58 words. Every sentence earns its place. Nothing needs to be explained twice.

## How to use this

**Standard increase (small, one line):** Claude drafts from the manufacturer's notice. You review, send to the base segment.

**Large increase (big number, many lines):** Use the decision-document-template first to think through what to communicate, when, and to whom. Then come back here to draft the actual letter.

**Timing:** Send 2-4 weeks before the effective date. Earlier risks being forgotten; later doesn't give contractors time to lock in.

---

## The rules

- **Name the manufacturer.** Don't hide behind "a key supplier" or "an industry partner." Contractors figure it out in 30 seconds and think less of you for hedging.
- **Name the real percentage.** "Modest price adjustment" is weasel. "7%" is honest. Honest is what contractors remember.
- **Never apologise for the increase you didn't cause.** You're passing it through, not imposing it. "Sorry for the inconvenience" is soft. "Here's the situation and here's what we can do about it" is respect.
- **Always offer a lock-in.** If you can protect a chunk of revenue by holding current pricing for 30 days on orders placed now, do it. Contractors remember.
- **Save letters sent:** `WORK AREAS/Customer-Accounts/price-communications-project/outputs/[Vendor]-[Line]_Price-Increase-Letter_[date].md`. Building a library speeds up the next one.

---

## After the letter goes out

Track two things:

1. **Stock-ahead response.** How much volume got pulled forward vs. your normal baseline? This tells you how well the campaign worked and calibrates the next one.
2. **Complaint and attrition response.** Which contractors pushed back hard or went quiet? Log in the contractor's account memory. If a top-25 account shifted spend after a specific increase, you need to know.
