# Email formats template

Use this when drafting any distributor email. Ten formats covered — five customer-facing, four vendor-facing, one internal. Tell Claude which format or just describe the situation and Claude will pick.

---

## What Claude needs from you

**The context:** Who is this? Contractor, service tech, key account, new account? Manufacturer rep, regional manager, buying group? If replying, paste the thread or point to the Gmail/Outlook conversation.

**What's happening:** One line on the situation. "Backorder on the Rheem line, three back-ordered jobs, ETA moved three weeks." "Carrier price increase letter arrived, need to push back."

**The ask:** What should the reader do after reading? Approve, schedule, pay, send a PO, accept the increase, reject it, show up to a QBR?

**Tone override:** If this needs something different from your default voice (set in About Me files), say so. "More formal — they're a GC's AP department." "Direct as hell — we've been jerked around on this."

## CUSTOMER-FACING EMAILS

### Format 1: Quote reply

**When:** You're sending a quote to a contractor who asked for pricing.

**Structure:**
1. One-line confirm what they asked for. "Pricing attached for the Westchester Medical rough-in, 14 fixtures."
2. Surface the one thing that matters — lead time, availability flag, or a substitution they need to see.
3. Point to the attached quote document (don't repeat line items in the email).
4. State the expiration: "Quote is good through May 15."
5. Clear next step: "Send the PO when ready — we can get it rolling the same day."

### Format 2: Back-order / fill-rate notification

**When:** A line on their open order just went back-order or the ETA slipped.

**Structure:**
1. Straight to the point. "Heads up — the Rheem XE50 on your PO 48221 moved from Monday delivery to April 22."
2. The reason, briefly, if it's real. "Factory allocation issue — it's industry-wide right now on this SKU."
3. What we're doing about it. Options, substitutions, transfers. Not apologies.
4. What they need to do, if anything. Confirm substitution, change the ship method, hold for the original SKU.
5. Name and phone for immediate response.

### Format 3: Price increase letter to contractor base

**When:** A manufacturer raised prices and you need to pass the increase.

**Structure:**
1. What's changing and when. Exact date. Exact lines affected.
2. Why, in one sentence. No vendor-blaming, no corporate-speak.
3. The math on their side. Typical % impact on their cost structure — optional, but appreciated.
4. Lock-in opportunity. "Orders placed and shipped by [date] hold current pricing."
5. Sign-off with a direct line.

Keep it under 200 words. Most contractors will skim.

### Format 4: Credit hold / collections — first notice

**When:** An invoice is 30-45 days past due and we need to say something before it gets awkward.

**Structure:**
1. Friendly, not accusatory. "Invoice #48221 from March 4 is outstanding at $12,480."
2. Brief acknowledgment — maybe they got busy, maybe there was a dispute we didn't hear about.
3. Specific ask. "Can you get payment out this week, or let us know if there's a question on the invoice?"
4. Offer to call. Phone and name.
5. No late-fee threats in the first notice.

(Escalation formats — demand letter, final notice — use the decision-document-template for the call first. Those go out rarely and carefully.)

### Format 5: Contractor onboarding / welcome

**When:** New customer, first order just shipped, first invoice going out.

**Structure:**
1. Welcome — brief, specific to them, not a form letter.
2. Who their rep is (name, direct line, email).
3. Who their branch contact is for same-day needs.
4. Payment terms confirmation from their credit app.
5. One thing they should know about us they probably didn't pick up in the sales process. "Our after-hours emergency line is 555-0144 — service plumbers use it constantly for Friday-night water heater calls."

## VENDOR-FACING EMAILS

### Format 6: Vendor negotiation / price pushback

**When:** Manufacturer announced an increase, you're pushing back.

**Structure:**
1. Acknowledge receipt — one sentence.
2. The facts: what we bought last year, what we committed to this year, rebate status, fill-rate performance from your end.
3. The ask. Be specific. "We need the increase delayed 60 days and held at 4% instead of 7% on the AC line." Not "we'd like to discuss."
4. What we'll do if they agree. Not threats — specifics. "We'll hold our stocking position on the Gold series through Q3 and bring in the new Carrier IQ pilot to all four branches."
5. Timeline for response: "I need an answer by Friday."

### Format 7: Vendor QBR prep — agenda send

**When:** 7-10 days before a QBR, sending the agenda so the vendor shows up prepared.

**Structure:**
1. Confirm date, time, attendees from both sides.
2. Agenda items — no more than 5, bulleted.
3. What we need from them. "Bring Q1 fill rate, open back-orders by SKU, and 2026 rebate attainment numbers."
4. What we'll bring. Shows it's a two-way review, not a one-way audit.
5. Pre-read attachments if any. Keep it under two documents.

### Format 8: Rebate claim / dispute

**When:** Filing a quarterly rebate claim or disputing a vendor's calculation.

**Structure:**
1. Program name, claim period, claim number.
2. Our number (what we think the rebate should be), with the PO detail backing it.
3. Their number (if disputing), and the delta.
4. Question or request. "Please confirm we'll be credited $18,400 by April 30" or "Please review and respond with the corrected amount."
5. Attach the supporting PO list, don't paste it in the email.

### Format 9: New product intake / evaluation

**When:** Vendor is pitching a new line, or you're signalling interest.

**Structure:**
1. What we've seen / heard.
2. Questions we need answered before stocking: pricing, MOQ, freight, rebate eligibility, warranty, marketing support, who's the first customer.
3. What would make us say yes. "If you can hit the Kohler Pro spec at 5% better and give us 90-day pay-when-sold on the initial stock, we'll bring it in at two branches for a 6-month pilot."
4. Who needs to be in the next conversation on our side.

## INTERNAL / TEAM EMAILS

### Format 10: Rep coaching note / territory update

**When:** Documenting a ride-along observation, a rep-level decision, or weekly territory note.

**Structure:**
1. Who, what, when, one line.
2. What happened — observation or issue or win.
3. What we're doing about it / what the rep should do differently.
4. Follow-up date. "I'll circle back on this at our 1:1 May 3."

## Good vs bad (distributor edition)

**Bad:**
"Dear Valued Customer, we are writing to inform you that due to unforeseen circumstances in the global supply chain, there has been a slight delay in the anticipated arrival of certain items on your recent order. We deeply apologize for any inconvenience this may cause and assure you that we are working diligently to resolve this matter. Thank you for your patience and continued partnership."

**Good:**
"Tom — heads up. The Rheem XE50 on PO 48221 moved from Monday to April 22. Factory allocation, industry-wide. I've got a Bradford White equivalent in stock if you need it sooner — same connections, same BTU, about $40 more per unit. Call me if you want to swap. Otherwise we'll hold the original. Sarah, 518-555-0142."

The good version has a name, a date, a reason, an option, a decision point, and a callback number. Total words: 52.
