### 2026-04-16 — Acme Plumbing (first QBR) — lessons

Category: Lessons learned

Ran the first QBR with Acme Plumbing. What worked: having the one-page summary in hand before the call kept us tight on time (35 min vs our usual 60). What didn't: we missed that their fill rate had dropped from 96% to 91% on the Rheem line. The contractor noticed before we did, because three back-orders cost him a water-heater install last week. Lesson: always run a fill-rate trend on the QBR report, not just the point-in-time number. Updated the template.

### 2026-04-16 — Fill-rate trend added to template

Category: System change

Added "Fill-rate trend: last 4 weeks" as a required field on the QBR one-page template. Also added a flag rule: if any vendor line's fill rate dropped more than 3 points in the last 4 weeks, highlight it in red so the rep walks into the meeting with the answer ready.

## Lessons learned

### Contractors track our performance whether we show them or not

Every contractor we've talked to so far has a mental model of "how good are these guys at fill rate right now?" If we don't bring that data, they will — and they'll remember specific shipments we got wrong. Better to bring the data and own the conversation.

### One clear ask beats three soft asks

The QBR template originally had a "next steps" section with 3-5 items. The first two QBRs proved this dilutes everything. Moving to one explicit ask (which can be a yes/no from the contractor) is sharper and generates more action.

## Blockers

Sarah's rep territory (8 of the 25 accounts) has a calendar overloaded with new-construction site visits. We're working around it by doing phone QBRs where the contractor prefers, and in-person only for the top 3 accounts in her territory.
