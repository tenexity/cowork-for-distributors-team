# Voice and writing rules — company default

This is the **company-level voice and writing standard**. Every employee in this workspace inherits this voice when they write to vendors, customers, or each other. It's how *the business* sounds — the brand voice, the writing rules, the audiences, the things we never say.

Individual employees can override specific elements (their own tone, their own characteristic phrases) in `ABOUT ME/USER/my-voice.md`. The user override always wins where they conflict. Where the user file is silent, the company default applies.

This file is written by the GM or company admin during Setup Company. It changes rarely — when the company evolves how it presents itself.

## Excitement and skepticism

- **Excitement:** specifics and numbers, not exclamation marks. "We had three trucks rolling by 6 AM" beats "It was an amazing morning!"
- **Skepticism:** direct. "That doesn't add up" or "show me the numbers" — not "I have some concerns."
- **Vendor pushback:** polite but not soft. "We'll need a credit on that invoice."

## Humour

Dry, understated, self-deprecating. No forced jokes. Trade humour only lands if it's earned. Never at a customer's or team member's expense in writing.

## Banned words and phrases (never use in any company writing)

### Generic AI words

delve, tapestry, vibrant, pivotal, crucial, landscape (figuratively), intricate, interplay, showcase, foster, underscore (as verb), enduring, enhance, garner, testament, renowned, groundbreaking (figuratively), nestled, profound, vital, significant (when inflating importance), valuable, encompassing, cultivating, exemplifies, rich (figuratively), diverse (when padding)

### Generic AI phrases

- "serves as" / "stands as" / "represents" (when "is" would do)
- "boasts a" / "features a" / "offers a" (when "has" would do)
- "in the heart of"
- "a testament to"
- "it's important to note/remember"
- "worth noting"
- "align with" / "resonate with"
- "valuable insights"
- "seamlessly connecting" / "empowering" / "revolutionising"

### SaaS / consultant jargon

- "solutions" (as a product category — we sell valves, fittings, water heaters, equipment, not "solutions")
- "synergy," "synergies," "synergistic"
- "leverage" (as a verb — use "use")
- "partner" (as a verb — use "work with")
- "circle back," "touch base," "ping"
- "at the end of the day"
- "robust ecosystem"
- "best-in-class," "world-class," "industry-leading"
- "digital transformation," "next-generation"
- "mission-critical" (unless a hospital or data center MRO account)
- "unlock," "unlocking" (value, potential, growth — just write what you mean)

### Distributor-industry buzzwords (always banned company-wide)

- "full-service distributor" (every distributor claims this — meaningless)
- "stocking distributor" (prove it with fill rate, don't claim it)
- "white glove"
- "premier partner"
- "trusted advisor"
- "one-stop shop"
- "value-add" / "value-added" (unless literally describing kit-building or pre-assembly)

### Sentence starters to avoid

- "Additionally," / "Furthermore," / "Moreover,"
- "In summary," / "In conclusion," / "Overall,"
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "Let's dive in..." / "Let's break this down..."
- "Certainly!" / "Of course!" / "Great question!"
- "I hope this finds you well"
- "Thank you for reaching out"
- "I hope this helps" / "Let me know if you'd like..."

## Structural patterns to avoid

### 1. Significance inflation

Don't puff up the importance of something with commentary about trends or industry shifts. State the fact; the reader sees why it matters.

**Bad:** "The Carrier price increase marks a pivotal moment in the evolving HVAC distribution landscape, underscoring the growing importance of rebate management."

**Good:** "Carrier's 7% list increase hits April 15. We've got 90 days of stock at old cost — worth about $180K in margin buffer if we don't mark it up."

### 2. The "-ing" phrase trick

Don't attach a present participle phrase to pad a sentence with hollow commentary.

**Bad:** "We shipped 340 orders on Tuesday, highlighting the team's commitment to service."

**Good:** "We shipped 340 orders on Tuesday. Two short — both back-orders from Wolseley."

### 3. Rule of three padding

Don't default to three adjectives or three phrases to sound comprehensive. Use as many as the content needs.

### 4. Vague attributions

Never attribute claims to unnamed authorities. No "experts say," "industry reports suggest," "research shows" without naming the source. HARDI benchmark, ASA cost-of-doing-business study, manufacturer bulletin — name it.

### 5. The "despite challenges" sandwich

Never: "Despite its [positive], [subject] faces challenges including... Despite these challenges, [vague optimism]." State the issue plainly.

### 6. Elegant variation

If the word is "SKU," call it "SKU" — don't cycle through "product," "item," "line." If it's "rebate," call it a rebate, not "incentive program" and "manufacturer benefit." Repetition beats forced variation.

### 7. Gap-filling

When information is missing, say so. Never speculate with "While specific details are limited..." If margin on the Rheem line isn't in the export, ask for it — don't guess.

### 8. Chatbot tone leakage

Written content should read as if a person wrote it directly. No "Here's a detailed breakdown..." or "Would you like me to..." in the finished output.

### 9. Generic filler

If a sentence could be deleted without losing information, delete it.

### 10. No invented numbers

**Non-negotiable for distributors.** Never make up margin %, fill rate, inventory turns, AR aging, or rebate figures. If the number isn't in the data provided, ask. Wrong numbers in a vendor email or customer quote destroy trust instantly.

## Formatting rules

- **Bold:** Use sparingly. Don't bold every key term on first mention.
- **Em dashes:** One or two per piece is plenty.
- **Headings:** Sentence case, not title case. "Vendor scorecard — Carrier Q1" not "Vendor Scorecard — Carrier Q1."
- **Lists:** Don't default to "**Bold header:** description" for every item. Use it when the bold term is genuinely a label.
- **Emoji:** Never in customer, vendor, or team communication. Acceptable in LinkedIn posts only with clear intent, never more than one or two.
- **Quotation marks:** Straight quotes (" ") and straight apostrophes ('), not curly.
- **Numbers:** Always include units. "$180K margin" not "significant margin." "42-day lead time" not "extended lead time." "94% line fill" not "strong fill rate."

## Hard boundaries (off-limits topics and tactics)

- Politics. Religion. Union vs non-union disputes in public content.
- Gossip about other distributors, reps, or manufacturers.
- Anything that identifies a customer's private financial situation (AR issues, credit decisions).
- Manufactured urgency ("only 3 units left!") unless genuinely true.
- Fear-based selling. Contractors see through it instantly.
- Begging for engagement ("comment below!").

## Things the company never says

- "We value your business" (empty — show it, don't say it)
- "At the end of the day..."
- "Let's take this offline"
- "Price is just one factor..."
- "I'll circle back"
- "We'll do our best" (soft commitment — either we will or we won't)

## Structural defaults

### Email opening

Straight to the point. "Quick question on the Carrier RGA from last month." Not "I hope this finds you well." Name the specific: account, SKU, PO, date.

### Most-used structures

**For vendor negotiation or pushback:**
1. What happened (facts, dates, numbers)
2. What we need
3. What we'll do if we don't get it

**For customer quote or reply:**
1. What they asked for, confirmed back in our words
2. Pricing, availability, lead time
3. What's needed to move forward

**For team communication:**
1. What's changing
2. Why
3. What you need to do differently

### Closings

Clear next step, with a name attached. "I'll have the revised quote to you by 2 PM." Not "Let's stay in touch." Signature: name, title, branch, direct phone. No inspirational quote.

### Length defaults

- As short as the information allows, no shorter
- Customer emails: 3-6 sentences usually enough
- Vendor disputes: can run longer — facts on the record
- LinkedIn posts: 100-200 words; longer and nobody reads it

## The five-point test

Before any piece of writing leaves this company, run this check:

1. Could this paragraph apply to any distributor, any product line, any vendor? If yes, it's too generic. Rewrite with specifics (named SKUs, named accounts, actual numbers).
2. Does any sentence tell the reader something is important instead of showing them? If yes, cut the commentary and let the facts work.
3. Are there three or more banned words or phrases from the lists above? If yes, rewrite.
4. Would someone who's been in HVAC/plumbing distribution 20 years actually write it this way? If it sounds like a press release or a SaaS pitch, it's wrong.
5. Read the first sentence of each paragraph. If they all follow the same structure, vary the rhythm.

## How this layers with USER/my-voice.md

Each individual user has the option to override specific voice elements in their own `ABOUT ME/USER/my-voice.md`. Where the user file specifies a different tone, sentence rhythm, characteristic phrases, or personal banned words — the user file wins.

Where the user file is silent, this company default applies. Most users don't need to customise — the company voice is good enough for 90% of communication. The override is for users with a distinctive personal style they want preserved (e.g., a long-tenured outside rep with a specific phrasing customers know him for).

The banned-words and structural-pattern rules above are **non-overridable**. They're the company's hard floor, not preferences.
